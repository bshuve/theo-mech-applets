Consider an object of mass \(m=1\) \(kg\) constrained to move in the \(y\)-direction under uniform gravitational acceleration \(-g\), where the gravitational constant \(g=9.8\) \(m/s^2\). The kinetic energy of the object is \(T=\frac{1}{2}m\dot{y}^2\) and the potential energy is \(U=mgy\). We consider the motion of the object from \(t=-\sqrt{\frac{2y_i}{g}}\) s to \(t=\sqrt{\frac{2y_i}{g}}\) s, with its position beginning and ending at \(y=0\). The position \(y_i=1\) \(m\) occurs when \(t=0\) \(s\).

In the applet below, the path is parametrized by the function \(y(t) = (y_i-\frac{1}{2}gt^2) + py_i\cos(\frac{\pi}{2}\frac{t}{t_0}) \) so that when \(p=0\), we have the classical path \(y(t) = y_i - \frac{1}{2}gt^2\).

Play around with the applet. It allows you to specify alternative paths that obey the boundary conditions specified in the problem and to visualize the object's motion over time. The red arrows represent the acceleration derived from the first derivative. The blue arrows represent the acceleration derived from using F/m.

Check out how the action and acceleration changes with p. Notice how the acceleration is different from F/m, even though they should be the same. When are these two values the same? When is action least? What does this suggest about the Principle of Least Action?
