Force vs. Acceleration: Page 5

Non-classical Spring

(Chrome Recommended.)

Consider an object of mass \(m = 5\) kg attached to a spring and streched in the x-direction under the condition \(x(t) = A\cos(\omega t)+B\sin(2\omega t)\), where the constants are defined below. Force applied to displace the mass is conservative, with initial value \(F_0 = kA\).
The action of the object is given by \(S = \int_{0}^{6 \pi/\omega} (T-U) \ dt \).

Try dragging the value of the \(B\)-slider in this applet to see how the animation and graphs change. Notice how the action \(S\) reaches a minimum when the path is classical.


Define Constants:

\(m\): 5.0 kg

\(k\): 5.0 N/m

\(\omega\): 1 s-1

\(A\): 0.1 m

Action \(S\):

.... Potential Energy

.... Negative Potential Energy

.... Kinetic Energy

Upper (green): \( U \)

Lower (grey): \( -U \)

Green: \( \partial U/ \partial y \)

Grey: \( -\partial U / \partial y \)






The Potential Derivative vs. Time graphs show the values of \(\frac{\partial U}{\partial x}\) and \(-\frac{\partial U}{\partial x}\). Because the force on the object is conservative, we know \(-\frac{\partial U}{\partial x} = F_x\) where \(F\) is the spring force.

When we compare the \(\frac{d}{dt}\) Kinetic Derivative \(\frac{d}{dt}(\frac{\partial T}{\partial \dot{x}})\) vs. Time (s) graph to the Negative Potential Derivative \(-\frac{\partial U}{\partial x}\) graph, we can see that the only \(B\) value that makes the two graphs the same and thus satisfies \(-\frac{\partial U}{\partial x} = \frac{d}{dt}(\frac{\partial T}{\partial \dot{x}})\) is \(B=0\).

As before, \(F=ma\) and the Lagrangian are only true for the classical path where \(B=0\).
