Non-classical Spring

(Chrome Recommended.)

Consider an object of mass \(m = 5\) kg attached to a spring and streched in the x-direction under the condition \(x(t) = Acos(\omega t)+Bsin(2\omega t)\), where the constants are defined below. The initial force applied to displace the mass is conservative, given by \(F = kA\).
The action of the object is given by \(S = \int_{0}^{6 \pi} (KE-PE) \ dt \).

Try dragging the value of the B-slider in this applet to see how the animation and graphs change. Notice how the action \(S\) reaches a minimum when the path is classical.


Define Constants:

\(m\): 5.0 kg

\(k\): 5.0 N/m

\(\omega\): 1 s-1

\(A\): 0.1 m

Action \(S\):

.... Potential Energy

.... Negative Potential Energy

.... Kinetic Energy





The Potential Derivative vs. Time graphs show the values of \(\frac{\partial U}{\partial y}\) and \(-\frac{\partial U}{\partial y}\). Because the force on the object is conservative, we know \(-\frac{\partial U}{\partial y} = F\) where \(F\) is the displacement force.

When we compare the d/dt Kinetic Derivative \(\frac{d}{dt}(\frac{\partial T}{\partial \dot{y}})\) vs. Time (s) graph to the Negative Potential Derivative \(-\frac{\partial U}{\partial y}\) graph, we can see that the only \(B\) value that makes the two graphs the same and thus satisfies \(-\frac{\partial U}{\partial y} = \frac{d}{dt}(\frac{\partial T}{\partial \dot{y}})\) is \(B=0\).

As before, \(F=ma\) and the Lagrangian are only true for the classical path where \(B=0\).
