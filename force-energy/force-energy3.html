Non-Uniform Acceleration

Consider an object of mass \(m=1.0\) kg constrained to move in the \(y\)-direction under gravitational acceleration \(-g\). The kinetic energy of the object is \(T=m\dot{y}^2/2\) and the potential energy is \(U=mgy\). In this case, the path is parametrized by the function \(y(t) = 1-t^p\).
The action of the object is given by \(S = \int_{0}^{4} (KE-PE) \ dt \).

Try dragging the value of the p-slider in this applet to see how the animation and graphs change. Notice how the action \(S\) reaches a minimum when the path is classical.

The Potential Derivative \((\frac{\partial U}{\partial y})\) vs. Time (s) graph shows the values of \(\frac{\partial U}{\partial y}\) and \(-\frac{\partial U}{\partial y}\). Because the force on the object is conservative, we know \(-\frac{\partial U}{\partial y} = F_y\). The force from Question 1 should match the value of the negative potential energy derivative on the graph. Note that the potential energy derivatives are time-independent because they are derived with respect to \(y\) position, and that the potential energy derivative graphs are not affected by changing \(p\).

Through the Kinetic Energy (J) vs. \(\dot{y}\) (m/s) graph, we can see that the relationship between \(T\) and \(\dot{y}\) is quadratic. When we look at the Kinetic Derivative \((\frac{\partial T}{\partial \dot{y}})\) vs. Time (s) graph though, we can see that the only \(p\) value that satisfies \(-\frac{\partial U}{\partial y} = \frac{d}{dt}(\frac{\partial T}{\partial \dot{y}})\) is \(p=2\).

As before, \(F=ma\) is only true for the classical path. When \(p=2\), \(a=-2\), which satisfies \(F=ma\).
