Non-Uniform Acceleration

(Chrome Recommended.)

Consider a similar setup to the last page (projectile motion under uniform gravitational acceleration \(-g\) from initial height \(h\)). However, we now parametrize the path of the object by the function \(y(t) = 1-t^p\).

Try dragging the value of the p-slider in this applet to see how the animation and graphs change. Notice how the action \(S\) reaches a minimum when the path is classical.

After testing different \(p\) values, try answering the following questions.


The object's acceleration is described by \(a(t) = -p(p-1) t^{p-2}\). In this scenario, the acceleration is not uniform unless \(p=2\).

Define Constants:

\(m\): 1.0 kg

\(g\): 2.0 m/s2

Action \(S\):

.... Potential Energy

.... Negative Potential Energy

.... Kinetic Energy

Upper (green): \( U \)

Lower (grey): \( -U \)

Upper (green): \( \partial U/ \partial y \)

Lower (grey): \( -\partial U / \partial y \)






Just like in the last example, the potential is independent of \(p\), and thus the value of \(p\) does not change the generalized force term of the Euler-Lagrange equation, \(\frac{\partial L}{\partial y}=-\frac{\partial U}{\partial y}=-mg\). In particular, this is a constant.

Although the relationship between \(T\) and \(\dot{y}\) is quadratic, the non-uniform acceleration means that \(\frac{d}{dt}\left(\frac{\partial T}{\partial\dot y}\right) = m\ddot{y}\) is no longer in general a constant. When we look at the Kinetic Derivative \((\frac{\partial T}{\partial \dot{y}})\) vs. Time (s) graph though, we can see that the only \(p\) value that satisfies \(-\frac{\partial U}{\partial y} = \frac{d}{dt}(\frac{\partial T}{\partial \dot{y}})\) is \(p=2\), which is the value of \(p\) that gives a constant acceleration.
