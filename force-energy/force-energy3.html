Satisfying the Euler-Lagrange Equations to Minimize Action

Example: Spring Force

We now perform a similar exercise with a spring obeying Hooke's Law. Consider a mass \(m\) moving in the \(x\)-direction under the influence of a spring of constant \(k\).





We know from the Euler-Lagrange equation that \(\frac{\partial L}{\partial x} = \frac{d}{dt}(\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{x}})\). We additionally know that \(L = T-U\), and that \(T = \frac{1}{2}m\dot{x}^2\), \(U = \frac{1}{2}k{x}^2\) for this system.

\(\frac{\partial L}{\partial x} = -\frac{\partial U}{\partial x} = -kx\)

\(\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{x}} = \frac{\partial T}{\partial \dot{x}} = m\dot{x} \implies \frac{d}{dt}(\frac{\partial T}{\partial \dot{x}}) = m\ddot{x}\).

Note that the Euler-Lagrange equation gives \(-\frac{\partial U}{\partial x} = \frac{d}{dt}(\frac{\partial T}{\partial \dot{x}})\).

We know that there is only the force associated with the spring potential is \(F_x=-\frac{\partial U}{\partial y}\), so the net force in the \(x\) direction is just \(F_x=-kx\) as well.

So, using the Euler-Lagrange equation, we can prove Newton's 2nd Law, \(F_x=-kx=m\ddot x\).
