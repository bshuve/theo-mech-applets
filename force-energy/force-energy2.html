Force vs. Acceleration: Page 2

Non-Classical Trajectory

Consider an object of mass \(m=1.0\) kg constrained to move in the \(y\)-direction under uniform gravitational acceleration \(-g\). The kinetic energy of the object is \(T=m\dot{y}^2/2\) and the potential energy is \(U=mgy\). We consider the motion of the object from \(t=-1\) s to \(t=+1\) s, with its position beginning and ending at \(y=0\). In this case, the path is parametrized by the function \(y(t) = (1+p)(1-t^2)\).

The Potential Derivative \((\frac{\partial U}{\partial y})\) vs. Time (s) graph shows the values of \(\frac{\partial U}{\partial y}\) and \(-\frac{\partial U}{\partial y}\). Because the force on the object is conservative, we know \(-\frac{\partial U}{\partial y} = F_y\). If you compare your force calculation from above, it should match the value of the negative potential energy derivative on the graph. Note that the potential energy derivatives are time-independent because they are derived with respect to \(y\) position, and that the potential energy derivative graphs are not affected by changing \(p\).

Through the Kinetic Energy (J) vs. \(\dot{y}\) (m/s) graph, we can see that the relationship between \(T\) and \(\dot{y}\) is quadratic. When we look at the Kinetic Derivative \((\frac{\partial T}{\partial \dot{y}})\) vs. Time (s) graph though, we can see that the slope of this line \(\frac{d}{dt}(\frac{\partial T}{\partial \dot{y}}\)) is only equal to the \(-\frac{\partial U}{\partial y}\) value on the left graph when \(p=0\). Thus, \(-\frac{\partial U}{\partial y} = \frac{d}{dt}(\frac{\partial T}{\partial \dot{y}})\) is only true when \(p=0\). We can think of \(p\) as representing deviation from the "classical" projectile motion trajectory.

By explicit differentiation, we obtain the object's acceleration in the y direction \(a=-2(1+p)\) which is only equal to the force \(F_y = -2.00\) when \(p=0\). This means that \(F=ma\) will only be true for the classical path.
