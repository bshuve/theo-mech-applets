The Potential Derivative \((\frac{\partial U}{\partial y})\) vs. Time (s) graph shows the values of \(\frac{\partial U}{\partial y}\) and \(-\frac{\partial U}{\partial y}\). Because the force on the object is conservative, we know \(-\frac{\partial U}{\partial y} = F = k \times A\). If you compare your force calculation from using the sliders, it should match the value of the negative potential energy derivative on the graph. Note that the potential energy derivatives are time-independent because they are derived with respect to \(y\) position. Through the Kinetic Energy (kJ) vs. \(\dot{y}\) (m/s) graph, we can see that the relationship between \(T\) and \(\dot{y}\) is quadratic. When we look at the Kinetic Derivative \((\frac{\partial T}{\partial \dot{y}})\) vs. Time (s) graph though, we can see that the slope of this line \(\frac{d}{dt}(\frac{\partial T}{\partial \dot{y}}\)) is equal to the \(-\frac{\partial U}{\partial y}\) value on the left graph. Thus, we can derive the connection that \(-\frac{\partial U}{\partial y} = \frac{d}{dt}(\frac{\partial T}{\partial \dot{y}})\).





We know from the Euler-Lagrange equation that \(\frac{\partial L}{\partial y} = \frac{d}{dt}(\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{y}})\). We additionally know that \(L = T-U\), and that \(T = \frac{1}{2}m\dot{y}^2\), \(U = mgy\) for this situation.

\(\frac{\partial L}{\partial y} = -\frac{\partial U}{\partial y} = F_y\) by definition.

\(\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{y}} = \frac{\partial T}{\partial \dot{y}} = m\dot{y} \implies \frac{d}{dt}(\frac{\partial T}{\partial \dot{y}}) = m\ddot{y} = F_y\) by definition.

Note that \(-\frac{\partial U}{\partial y} = \frac{d}{dt}(\frac{\partial T}{\partial \dot{y}})\) as seen in the graphs.

So, using the Euler-Langrange equation, we can prove Newton's 2nd Law, \(F=ma\).
