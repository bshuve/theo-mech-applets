We know from the Euler-Lagrange equation that \(\frac{\partial L}{\partial x} = \frac{d}{dt}(\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{x}})\). We additionally know that \(L = T-U\), and that \(T = \frac{1}{2}m\dot{x}^2\), \(U = \frac{1}{2}k{x}^2\) for this situation.

\(\frac{\partial L}{\partial x} = -\frac{\partial U}{\partial x} = kx\)

\(\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{x}} = \frac{\partial T}{\partial \dot{x}} = m\dot{x} \implies \frac{d}{dt}(\frac{\partial T}{\partial \dot{x}}) = m\ddot{x} = F_x\) by definition.

We know that there is only the spring potential energy force from the initial applied force to stretch the spring acting on the system, so the net force in the \(x\) direction is just \(F_x=kx\) by definition as well.

Note that \(-\frac{\partial U}{\partial x} = \frac{d}{dt}(\frac{\partial T}{\partial \dot{x}})\) as seen in the graphs.

So, using the Euler-Lagrange equation, we can prove Newton's 2nd Law, \(F=ma\).
