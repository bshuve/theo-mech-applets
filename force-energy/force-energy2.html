Satisfying the Euler-Lagrange Equations to Minimize Action

Example: Non-Uniform Acceleration

We consider the same physical system as the last page, but we now consider a different family of alternate paths parametrized by \(y(t) = \frac{1}{2}g(1-t^p/t_0^p)\), where again we consider \(p\) as a parameter to vary.


The object's acceleration is described by \(a(t) = -\frac{1}{2}gp(p-1) t^{p-2}/t_0^p\). In this scenario, the acceleration is not uniform unless \(p=2\).

As on the last page, you can adjust the value of \(p\) in the applet below and observe the changes in the derivatives of potential and kinetic energy.

Define Constants:

\(m\): 1.0 kg

\(g\): 2.0 m/s2

Action (J.s):

.... Potential Energy

.... Negative Potential Energy

.... Kinetic Energy

Upper (green): \( \partial U/ \partial y \)

Lower (grey): \( -\partial U / \partial y \)






Although the potential depends on \(y\), its derivative simply equals \(mg\) and does not depend on \(y\) (or, for that matter, \(p\)).

The non-uniform acceleration means that \(\frac{d}{dt}\left(\frac{\partial T}{\partial\dot y}\right) = m\ddot{y}\) is no longer in general a constant. We see that the only \(p\) value satisfying \(-\frac{\partial U}{\partial y} = \frac{d}{dt}(\frac{\partial T}{\partial \dot{y}})\) is \(p=2\), which corresponds to a constant, non-zero acceleration.
