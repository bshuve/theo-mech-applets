Satisfying the Euler-Lagrange Equations to Minimize Action

In this applet, we explore the connection between the action of a path and whether the path satisfies the Euler-Lagrange equations. We will go through a few specific systems that are familiar to you from mechanics.

As a reminder, the Euler-Lagrange equation for a coordinate \(q\) is \(\frac{d}{dt}\left(\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot q}\right) = \frac{\partial L}{\partial q}\). For a given system, we can check that the Euler-Lagrange equations are satisfied for a particular path by calculating the left- and right-hand sides of the equation and confirming that they are equal to one another.

For this applet, we restrict ourselves to Cartesian coordinates. In this case, the kinetic energy \(T = \frac{1}{2}mv^2\) does not depend explicitly on any coordinate, and the potential energy \(U\) is independent of velocity. Consequently, we can write \(\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot x} = \frac{\partial T}{\partial \dot x}\) and \(\frac{\partial L}{\partial x} = -\frac{\partial U}{\partial x}\), and similarly for the other coordinates.

The Euler-Lagrange equation is then \(\frac{d}{dt}\left(\frac{\partial T}{\partial \dot x}\right) = -\frac{\partial U}{\partial x}\). We can use this to check the equivalence of the Euler-Lagrange equations and Newton's Second Law for Cartesian coordinates.



We know from the Euler-Lagrange equation that \(\frac{\partial L}{\partial y} = \frac{d}{dt}(\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{y}})\). We additionally know that \(L = T-U\), and that \(T = \frac{1}{2}m\dot{y}^2\), \(U = mgy\) for this situation.

\(\frac{\partial L}{\partial y} = -\frac{\partial U}{\partial y} = -mg = F_y\) by definition for a conservative force.

\(\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{y}} = \frac{\partial T}{\partial \dot{y}} = m\dot{y} \implies \frac{d}{dt}(\frac{\partial T}{\partial \dot{y}}) = m\ddot{y} = F_y\) by definition.

Note that \(-\frac{\partial U}{\partial y} = \frac{d}{dt}(\frac{\partial T}{\partial \dot{y}})\) as seen in the graphs.

So, using the Euler-Lagrange equation, we can prove Newton's 2nd Law, \(F_y=ma_y\).

Consider an object of mass \(m=1.0\) kg constrained to move in the \(y\)-direction under uniform gravitational acceleration \(g\). We consider the motion of the object from \(t_i=-1\) s to \(t_f=+1\) s, with its position beginning and ending at \(y=0\). To make the numerics simpler, we take \(g=2\,\,\mathrm{m}/\mathrm{s}^2\).

The classical path is \(y(t) = \frac{1}{2}g(1-t^2/t_0^2)\) with \(t_0=1\,\,\mathrm{s}\). We then consider an alternate family of paths \(y(t) = \frac{1}{2}g(1+p)(1-t^2/t_0^2)\), which is equivalent to changing magnitude of the uniform acceleration. The alternate path only matches the classical path when \(p=0\).

In the applet below, you can change the value of \(p\). We display graphs of \(\frac{\partial U}{\partial y}\) and \(\frac{\partial T}{\partial \dot y}\) as functions of time for the indicated path, along with the time derivative (slope) of \(\frac{\partial T}{\partial \dot y}\).
