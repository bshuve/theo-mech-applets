How Lagrange's equations are equivalent to Newton's 2nd Law

Consider an object of mass \(m\) constrained to move in the \(y\)-direction under uniform acceleration \(a\) or \(\ddot{y}\). Because there is no \(x\) component of the acceleration in this case, you can assume that \(a\) refers to \(\ddot{y}\) whenever used on this page. The object is launched from height \(h\) so that it experiences projectile motion. The kinetic energy of the object is \(T = \frac{1}{2}m\dot{y}^2\) and the potential energy is \(U=may\). We consider the path of the object, with its position beginning at \(y=h\) and ending when it lands on the ground at \(y=0\).

We know from the Euler-Lagrange equation that \(\frac{\partial L}{\partial y} = \frac{d}{dt}(\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{y}})\). We additionally know that \(L = T-U\), and that \(T = \frac{1}{2}m\dot{y}^2\), \(U = may\) for this situation.

\(\frac{\partial L}{\partial y} = -\frac{\partial U}{\partial y} = F_y\) by definition.

\(\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{y}} = \frac{\partial T}{\partial \dot{y}} = m\dot{y} \implies \frac{d}{dt}(\frac{\partial T}{\partial \dot{y}}) = m\ddot{y} = F_y\) by definition.

Note that \(-\frac{\partial U}{\partial y} = \frac{d}{dt}(\frac{\partial T}{\partial \dot{y}})\).

So, using the Euler-Langrange equation, we can prove that \(F=ma\), connecting our new findings in theoretical mechanics to what we learned from Newton's Laws!

The graphs above help demonstrate this connection as follows.

The Potential Derivative \((\frac{\partial U}{\partial y})\) vs. Time (s) graph shows the values of \(\frac{\partial U}{\partial y}\) and \(-\frac{\partial U}{\partial y}\), which we know is equal to the force. If you compare your force calculation from using the sliders, it should match the value of the lines created from the graph. The potential energy derivatives are time-independent because they are derived with respect to \(y\) position. Through the Kinetic Energy (kJ) vs. \(\dot{y}\) (m/s) graph, we can see that the relationship between \(T\) and \(\dot{y}\) is quadratic. When we look at the Kinetic Derivative \(\frac{\partial T}{\partial \dot{y}}\) vs. Time (s) graph though, we can see that the slope of this line \(\frac{d}{dt}(\frac{\partial T}{\partial \dot{y}}\)) is equal to the \(-\frac{\partial U}{\partial y}\) value to the left graph, as derived above.
