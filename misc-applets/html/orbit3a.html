Orbital Motion and the Principle of Least Action

This applet visualizes the motion of a planet orbiting a star, demonstrating the physical principles that govern its trajectory. By adjusting key orbital parameters, you can explore how energy and angular momentum shape the path of a celestial body.

In classical mechanics, the motion of two gravitationally interacting bodies—like the Earth and the Sun—can be complex. To simplify this, we can reframe it as an equivalent one-body problem. This is achieved using the concept of reduced mass, denoted by the symbol μ (mu), where:

μ = (m₁m₂)/(m₁ + m₂)

This allows us to describe the motion of one body relative to the other in a much simpler way.

We can get the shape of the orbital path of μ as a function of the angle θ as follows:

r(θ) = (L²)/(μGm₁m₂) × 1/(1 + ε cos θ)

Here, ε is the orbital eccentricity, a parameter that defines the orbit's shape. When ε < 1, the path is a bound, elliptical orbit. While r(θ) describes the path of the reduced mass, the actual paths of the two individual bodies are given by:

r₁(θ) = (m₂)/(m₁+m₂) × r(θ) and r₂(θ) = -(m₁)/(m₁+m₂) × r(θ)

The total energy E of the system is also useful to understanding the behavior of the orbit. This energy is composed of three parts:

E = ½μṙ² + L²/(2μr²) - Gm₁m₂/r

The Radial Kinetic Energy is the energy of motion directly towards or away from the center of mass. The Angular Kinetic Energy, is the energy of the orbital motion. Notice how this term has a dependence on r and not ṙ. As r gets close to 0, there is an increasing outward force, preventing the orbit from getting too close to the center of mass. This is known as the centrifugal barrier.

Notice how the angular kinetic energy and gravitational potential energy both have a dependency on r. These last two terms are often combined into an Effective Potential (U_eff), which we use to understand the shape of the trajectory:

U_eff(r) = L²/(2μr²) - Gm₁m₂/r

This applet models a star-planet system where the star's mass (m₁) is much greater than the planet's (m₂), similar to our Sun and Earth.




When m₁ ≫ m₂, the denominator m₁ + m₂ is approximately equal to m₁. So, μ ≈ (m₁m₂)/m₁, which simplifies to μ ≈ m₂. This means the reduced mass is nearly identical to the planet's mass. As a result, the motion will follow the Earth around the center of mass, which will be mostly the Sun.
