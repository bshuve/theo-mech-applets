Conserved Quantities: Energy and Angular Momentum Control

In this final exploration, we take direct control of the two fundamental conserved quantities in orbital mechanics: total energy (E) and angular momentum (L). Unlike the previous pages where we adjusted derived parameters, here we manipulate the underlying physical quantities that govern all orbital motion.

The previous pages explored how individual orbital parameters affect motion. Now we examine the most fundamental approach: directly setting the conserved quantities of the system. In orbital mechanics, energy and angular momentum are conserved throughout the motion, meaning they remain constant as the planet orbits. These two quantities completely determine the orbital characteristics.

The total energy E determines whether an orbit is bound (E < 0) or unbound (E ≥ 0). For bound orbits, more negative energy means a more tightly bound system. The energy equation we explored earlier: \[ E = \frac{1}{2} \mu \dot{r}^2 + \frac{L^2}{2\mu r^2} - \frac{G m_1 m_2}{r} \] shows how energy is distributed between radial motion, angular motion, and gravitational potential throughout the orbit.

The angular momentum L represents the "amount of rotation" in the system and is conserved due to the central nature of gravitational force. It directly influences the orbital size and prevents the orbiting body from spiraling into the central mass - this is the centrifugal barrier we discussed earlier.

What makes this approach powerful is that E and L completely determine the orbital parameters. Once you set these conserved quantities, the minimum radius and eccentricity are automatically calculated: \[ r_{\text{min}} = \frac{-G m_1 m_2 + \sqrt{(G m_1 m_2)^2 + 2EL^2/\mu}}{2E} \] \[ \epsilon = \sqrt{1 + \frac{2EL^2}{\mu(G m_1 m_2)^2}} \]




When E becomes less negative (approaches zero), the eccentricity increases, making the orbit more elongated. This occurs because less negative energy means the system is less tightly bound. The planet can travel farther from the star before gravity pulls it back, creating a more stretched ellipse. At E = 0, the orbit becomes parabolic (ε = 1), representing the boundary between bound and unbound motion.

This page demonstrates the most fundamental level of orbital control. As you adjust E and L, observe these key relationships:

  • Energy-Eccentricity Relationship: Watch how making energy less negative (closer to zero) increases eccentricity, stretching the orbit.
  • Angular Momentum Effects: See how higher L values create larger orbits and lower eccentricities at the same energy.
  • Automatic Parameter Calculation: Notice how r_min and ε are derived quantities that respond to your fundamental choices of E and L.
  • Energy Distribution: Observe how the total energy gets redistributed between kinetic and potential forms as the planet moves through its orbit.
  • Effective Potential Shape: Watch how changing E and L reshapes the effective potential curve, determining the allowed range of motion.
