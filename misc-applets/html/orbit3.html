Orbital Motion and the Principle of Least Action

This applet visualizes the motion of a planet orbiting a star. It allows students to explore how orbital parameters like angular momentum and eccentricity affect the trajectory and how the principle of least action can be used to determine the path of a two-body system.

In classical mechanics, the motion of two gravitationally interacting bodies (like the Earth and the Sun) can be reduced to a one-body problem using the reduced mass \(\mu = \frac{m_1 m_2}{m_1 + m_2}\). The effective potential in this system is given by: \[ U_{\text{eff}}(r) = \frac{L^2}{2 \mu r^2} -\frac{G m_1 m_2}{r} \] where \(L\) is the angular momentum of the system. This potential includes both the gravitational attraction and the centrifugal barrier due to rotation.

The resulting orbital path can be derived from Newton’s Laws or from minimizing the action: \[ r(\theta) = \frac{L^2}{\mu Gm_1m_2}\frac{1}{1 + \epsilon \cos \theta} \] where \(\epsilon\) is the orbital eccentricity. In the bound case where \(\epsilon < 1\), ellipses will form. However, this is the path of the reduced mass. The path's of \(m_1\) and \(m_2\) will take on the following paths: \[ r_{m_1}(\theta)=\frac{m_2}{m_1+m_2}r \] \[ r_{m_2}(\theta)=-\frac{m_1}{m_1+m_2}r. \]

Let's look at system where \(m_1 >> m_2\), such as a star-planet system.



When \(m_1 >> m_2\), \(\mu\) will get close to \(\frac{m_1 m_2}{m_1}\), which simplifies to \( m_2\), giving us an elliptical equation that simulates \(m_2\), or \(m_{earth}\) orbitting around a very heavy mass. This makes sense with the radius equations, as \(r_1\) will be very close to 0 and \(r_2\) will be very close to \(r\).

In this applet, we model this using the masses of the Sun and Earth for \(m_1\) and \(m_2\) respectively. Let's call these \(m_{sun}\) and \(m_{earth}\).The sliders below let you control both \(\epsilon\) and angular momentum \(L\), allowing you to see how the orbit changes in real time. You’ll also see graphs showing position, angle, and energy over time.

Try adjusting the sliders to see how higher angular momentum stabilizes the orbit and how increasing eccentricity makes it more elongated.
