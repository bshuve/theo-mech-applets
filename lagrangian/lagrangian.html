Generalized Coordinate Lagrangians:

Warmup

Let's take a path that goes from \(r_i = (x_1,y_1)\) to \(r_f = (x_2,y_2)\). There are an infinite ways of parametrize this path. However, let's take two example parametrizations: \(r_i + t(r_f-r_i), t= [0,1]\) and \(r_i + (1-e^{-s})(r_f-r_i), s= [0,\infty)\)
Let's prove that the Principle of Least Action is the same for both parametrizations of the path! Suppose a constant force \( \vec{F}_0 \) acts along a path from an initial point \( \vec{r}_i = (x_1, y_1) \) to a final point \( \vec{r}_f = (x_2, y_2) \). Define the displacement vector: \[ \vec{R} = \vec{r}_f - \vec{r}_i \]

We consider two different parameterizations of the same path:

Parametrization A: A linear interpolation between \( \vec{r}_i \) and \( \vec{r}_f \): \[ \vec{r}(t) = \vec{r}_i + t\vec{R}, \quad t \in [0, 1] \] The velocity is: \[ \frac{d\vec{r}}{dt} = \vec{R} \] The work done by the force is: \[ W_A = \int_0^1 \vec{F}_0 \cdot \frac{d\vec{r}}{dt} \, dt = \vec{F}_0 \cdot \vec{R} \int_0^1 dt = \vec{F}_0 \cdot \vec{R} \] Parametrization B: An exponential approach to \( \vec{r}_f \): \[ \vec{r}(s) = \vec{r}_i + \left(1 - e^{-s} \right)\vec{R}, \quad s \in [0, \infty) \] The velocity is: \[ \frac{d\vec{r}}{ds} = e^{-s} \vec{R} \] The work done by the force is: \[ W_B = \int_0^\infty \vec{F}_0 \cdot \frac{d\vec{r}}{ds} \, ds = \vec{F}_0 \cdot \vec{R} \int_0^\infty e^{-s} ds = \vec{F}_0 \cdot \vec{R} \] Thus, In both parameterizations, the work done is: \[ W = \vec{F}_0 \cdot (\vec{r}_f - \vec{r}_i) \] This confirms that the work done by a constant force is independent of the parameterization of the pathâ€”it depends only on the endpoints. This is consistent with the principle of least action, which assures us that for conservative systems, physical paths minimize (or extremize) the action, and different parametrizations of the same path yield the same result.
